## The framing question

The data in §5.1 closes off the simplest reading of Romania's education
system — that some students do well and some do poorly, in a distribution
broadly normal for a middle-income EU country. That is not what the
evidence shows. Instead, the system produces two distinct outputs:

- A bottom of the distribution that is unusually deep — 49% of 15-year-olds
  below baseline mathematics proficiency, the largest rural-urban gap in
  the EU, functional illiteracy rising to 73% among rural high-schoolers.
- A top of the distribution that, when measured by elite competition
  results, sits among the most productive in the world: 5th worldwide on
  IMO medals per million inhabitants, top-10 worldwide for 60 years,
  consistent IOI placement.

The first track is the subject of §5.1-5.3. This section is about the
second.

The framing question for §5.4 is not whether the elite track exists — the
evidence is overwhelming that it does. The question is *how* it works, who
it serves, and what role it plays in the political economy of the whole
system.

## The Olympiad evidence

The single hardest evidence for the elite track is competition results
that allow direct cross-country comparison.

**International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO).** Romania hosted the first
IMO in 1959. Across 65 cycles through 2024, the country has accumulated:

- **88 gold medals, 161 silver, 112 bronze, 361 total** (Wikipedia, citing
  IMO official results)
- Average country ranking 7.5; consistent top-10 placement
- Ranked **#6 worldwide all-time** by gold medals — ahead of Vietnam, the
  UK, Bulgaria, Germany, Iran, Japan, Taiwan, Ukraine
- 2022 cycle: 1st in Europe, 5th worldwide ("best performance in 25 years"
  per *Romania Insider*)

When normalised by current population, Romania ranks **5th worldwide** at
19.0 medals per million inhabitants — ahead of every major power (the US
at 0.9, China at 0.2, Russia at 1.3, Germany at 3.1, the UK at 4.6) and
behind only Bulgaria (49.1), Hungary (40.0), Singapore (30.5), and Israel
(20.7). See `figures/05_imo_medals_per_capita.png`.

**International Olympiad in Informatics (IOI).** Romania first entered in
1990. Across 36 cycles:

- **38 gold, 60 silver, 37 bronze, 135 total**
- Ranked **#6 worldwide by total medals** as of 2026
- Zero honourable mentions — every Romanian who finishes the contest
  reaches the medal threshold. This perfect-conversion rate is rare and
  suggests the Romanian selection pipeline is unusually well-calibrated.

The per-capita Olympiad performance is the single hardest piece of
evidence for the brief's two-track thesis. A country whose schooling
system fails 49% of 15-year-olds on the most basic math proficiency cannot
*also* be running an inferior elite track. The elite track is real and
internationally competitive.

## The operational base of the elite track

The Olympiad results are the visible output. The operational base — the
schools, teachers, families, and informal networks that produce that
output — is less well-documented but follows a clear pattern.

### The *colegii naționale*

A small set of named urban high schools concentrates most of the
medallists. The Olympiad-feeding *colegii naționale* most commonly cited
in Romanian education journalism (Edupedu, *colegii naționale* rankings):

- **Colegiul Național de Informatică "Tudor Vianu", București** — the
  flagship informatics school, primary IOI feeder.
- **Colegiul Național "Mihai Viteazul", București**
- **Colegiul Național "Gheorghe Lazăr", București**
- **Colegiul Național "Sfântul Sava", București**
- **Colegiul Național "Emil Racoviță", Cluj-Napoca**
- **Liceul Teoretic Internațional "Onisifor Ghibu", Oradea**
- **Colegiul Național "C. Negruzzi", Iași**
- **Colegiul Național "Iași" (NCI), Iași**
- **Colegiul Național "Mircea cel Bătrân", Constanța**

The list is not exhaustive and the relative weight of each school varies
year to year. What matters analytically is that the list is *short* —
perhaps 20-30 schools out of roughly 1,400 high schools in the system
produce the great majority of national-team members. The remaining ~1,370
high schools, including the entire rural cohort, contribute almost nothing
to the Olympiad pipeline.

The brief should resist romanticising these schools. Their performance is
real, but their student intake is heavily filtered: they admit on the
basis of Evaluare Națională scores, with cutoff thresholds that reliably
select for students whose families have the resources to support
preparation (see *meditațiile*, below). The schools do not transform
average students into Olympiad competitors; they receive students who
already arrive equipped for that level of work, and then deepen the
preparation.

### The Olympiad infrastructure

Around the *colegii naționale*, a small ecosystem of supplementary
institutions:

- **Centrele de excelență.** County-level (*județean*) coordination
  centres for Olympiad-track students, formally part of the Ministry's
  CJRAE (*Centrul Județean de Resurse și Asistență Educațională*)
  structure but operationally somewhat independent.
- **Summer camps and training programmes.** Run by the Romanian Society
  of Mathematical Sciences (SSMR) and equivalents for informatics
  (Asociația Aspe, *infooltenia*, *infoeducatie*), often funded by a mix
  of ministerial grant and private sponsorship.
- **Individual coach networks.** A small number of named teachers — many
  with their own Olympiad backgrounds — function as the operational core
  of the system. Some are formally attached to a *colegiu național*;
  others work independently and consult across schools.
- **Online platforms.** The *infoarena* community is the canonical
  example: an open programming-contest platform built and maintained by
  Romanian alumni, used as both training resource and selection sieve.

The OECD 2025 review does not discuss any of this. The QX report
acknowledges Olympiads at the system level but is critical (see below).
Both reports treat the elite track as something the system *has* rather
than something the system *does* — i.e., as an exogenous resource rather
than a deliberately produced output. The brief should treat it as the
latter.

### Why informatics in particular

Romania's IOI strength is greater than its absolute IMO strength (the IMO
is older and competition broader, but per-cycle Romanian IOI performance
is more concentrated at the gold end). Four reinforcing factors:

1. **Heritage of strong school mathematics.** Romanian secondary
   mathematics teaching has, for historical reasons going back to the
   interwar period, emphasised problem-solving and proof at levels
   uncommon in Western European curricula. This base translates directly
   into IMO and informatics-Olympiad preparation.
2. **Low capital requirements.** Unlike laboratory science Olympiads,
   IOI training requires only a computer and an internet connection.
   This makes the elite-track infrastructure cheap to scale to motivated
   students even in lower-income urban areas, *conditional* on the
   student already having the mathematical foundation.
3. **Clear international benchmark.** IOI provides annual, objective,
   internationally comparable results. The visibility creates feedback
   loops for funding and prestige that, e.g., humanities Olympiads do not.
4. **Industrial pull.** From the late 1990s onward, the Romanian software
   industry — and the multinational R&D centres that opened in the 2000s —
   created a clear post-Olympiad career path. The elite track and the
   industry pipeline became mutually reinforcing (§5.5 returns to this).

## The shadow education system — *meditațiile*

Private tutoring (*meditațiile*) is the most underdocumented mechanism in
the Romanian system. It is also one of the most important.

What is known:

- *Meditațiile* are normalised and widespread. Urban middle-class
  households routinely budget for private tutoring from primary school
  onward, intensifying around the high-stakes Evaluare Națională (grade
  8) and Bacalaureat (grade 12).
- Household expenditure on *meditațiile* is not systematically measured.
  INS household budget surveys do not break this category out cleanly;
  estimates rely on small-scale surveys (CEAE, individual academic
  studies).
- QX is explicit that *meditațiile* "can unduly affect the school process"
  and proposes regulation — specifically, prohibiting *meditații* during
  standard school hours and managing conflicts of interest (where a
  teacher tutors students they also teach).
- The function of *meditațiile* in the elite track is twofold: (a)
  preparation for Evaluare Națională and Bacalaureat at a level the
  in-school curriculum does not reach, and (b) preparation for Olympiad
  competition where the gap between curriculum and contest is wider still.

The brief's interpretive claim about *meditațiile* is straightforward but
politically uncomfortable: **the Romanian elite track does not work
without them.** The *colegii naționale* admit students who have already
been prepared by years of tutoring. The Olympiad competitors have, almost
without exception, supplementary instruction beyond what their *colegiu*
provides. The shadow education system is not a peripheral phenomenon; it
is the operational connective tissue of the elite track.

This matters analytically because the brief's question — why the system
produces both mass failure and elite success — has a partial answer here.
The elite track functions because parents who can afford it pay for an
informal parallel system that the public system does not provide.
Households unable to pay for *meditațiile* are functionally excluded from
the elite track, regardless of their children's underlying ability.

## The QX critique — *olimpiodemie*

QX is, on the elite track, both supportive and critical. Supportive
because David himself acknowledges the system "can work at very high
levels when the conditions are right". Critical because the system has
produced, in his framing, an *olimpiodemie* — an epidemic of competitions:

- The Ministry funds, supports, or recognises over **500 olympics,
  contests, and competitions** annually as of 2025 — at every level from
  primary to high school, in every subject.
- This volume creates pressure on the regular instruction process,
  consumes scarce financial and instructional resources, and polarises
  the system between "olympians" and "functional illiterates" with little
  middle.

QX's proposed reform is to rationalise the count: one ministry-funded
Olympiad per discipline, with additional contests organised by partner
scientific organisations under ministry endorsement but not ministry
budget. The reform also prohibits primary-level Olympiads under ministry
authority, on grounds that competition pressure at primary risks
distorting the foundational competencies.

The brief should engage seriously with the *olimpiodemie* critique. It is
not a denial of the elite track's value; it is a claim that the elite
track has scaled past the point of marginal returns, and that the marginal
hour of instructional time spent on competition prep is now coming at the
expense of the mass-track instruction the system is failing to deliver.

## What the elite track is — and is not — evidence of

The brief's broader argument depends on what the elite track's existence
*does not* prove. Three demarcations:

1. **The elite track is not evidence of a working school system on
   average.** The §5.1 finding is independent. PISA, BRIO, and Eurostat
   measure the bulk of the distribution; Olympiad results measure the
   right tail. The two are mathematically compatible: a system can have
   both an elite tail and a mass failure.

2. **The elite track is not evidence that "the system has it in itself"
   to scale.** The elite track works because it operates outside the
   standard system, on a selected subpopulation, with informal
   supplementary instruction. The conditions that make it work do not
   generalise to the full student population without massive additional
   investment — and the investment so far has gone to the elite track,
   not to expanding the conditions to a wider population.

3. **The elite track is not evidence of a uniformly meritocratic
   selection process.** The selection looks meritocratic — it uses
   objective national exams (Evaluare Națională, Olympiad rounds) — but
   the meritocracy is conditional on prior access to private tutoring
   and to high-quality urban schools. Children whose parents can afford
   *meditațiile* and live in cities with *colegii naționale* are not
   selected against their merit; they are selected because the system
   reliably produces merit *in them*, and reliably does not in everyone
   else.

   The cleanest direct measurement of this is PISA's *academic
   resilience* indicator — the share of students in the bottom quarter
   of the country's socio-economic distribution who score in the top
   quarter of the country's mathematics performance. Both quartiles are
   computed within-country, so the measure is not contaminated by
   national wealth differences. In Romania the resilient share is
   **6.6%**, against an OECD average of roughly 10% and a chance-level
   baseline of 25%. Romania is **second-lowest in the OECD/CEE
   PISA-participating group, ahead only of Slovakia (6.1%)** (OECD 2025
   review, Fig. 4.12). The implication is direct: even within Romania's
   own distribution, the system fails to lift its disadvantaged
   students into the top group. The elite track is built almost
   exclusively from students who arrived in school already advantaged.

## Connecting to the rest of the brief

§5.5 takes the elite track's output and follows it forward into
universities and the IT industry. §5.9 (recommendations) returns to the
two-track equilibrium and asks whether the brief should recommend changes
to the elite-track infrastructure — for example, the QX *olimpiodemie*
reform — even if it produces no immediate gain in the mass-track
indicators. The brief's working position is that the elite track is a
genuine national asset and should be preserved, but that the policy
oxygen currently going to it has crowded out reform that could move the
mass-track numbers. Whether to make that trade-off explicit is the
political question §5.9 will ask.
