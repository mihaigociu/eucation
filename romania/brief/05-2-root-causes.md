# §5.2 — Root Causes

> **Status:** first draft, May 2026.
> **Workstream:** §5.2 of `analysis-plan.md`.
> **Primary sources:** OECD (2025) *Education and Skills in Romania*; David
> (2025) *Raportul QX*; UNICEF + FRA on Roma; World Vision Romania (2024)
> on rural welfare; Eurostat funding statistics.

---

## How this section is organised

The failure documented in §5.1 has no single cause. It has a small number of
*mechanisms* that compound. This section groups them into seven layers,
arranged from proximate to structural. Each layer's evidence is summarised
once; the brief returns to specific layers in §5.7 (case studies) and §5.9
(recommendations).

The layering is the brief's own — not QX's, not OECD's. Both source reports
organise their diagnosis differently (QX by domain, OECD by Quality / Equity
/ Governance pillar). The seven-layer version below is designed to make the
brief's later recommendation logic explicit: each recommendation in §5.9 is
mapped back to the layer it is meant to act on.

## Layer 1 — Household

The PISA finding is unambiguous: after controlling for student and school
socio-economic status, Romania's rural-urban gap is no longer statistically
significant. The school system is not creating the geography of failure; it
is reproducing the geography of poverty.

PISA's *academic resilience* metric tightens the point further. Of
Romanian students in the bottom quarter of the country's SES distribution,
only **6.6%** reach the top quarter of national math performance — vs an
OECD average of ~10% and a chance baseline of 25%. Romania is
second-lowest in the OECD/CEE PISA-participating group, ahead only of
Slovakia at 6.1% (OECD 2025, Fig. 4.12). Both numerator and denominator
are intra-country, so the result cannot be explained by Romania being a
middle-income country. The school system, measured against its *own*
distribution, transmits parental SES to academic outcomes more strongly
than most OECD peers.

The household-level drivers in evidence:

- **Poverty.** Romania has the EU's highest child poverty rate (Eurostat,
  via OECD 2025). More than half of rural Romanians are at risk of poverty
  or social exclusion. The brief does not need to argue this; it is the
  baseline condition every other mechanism operates on top of.
- **Parental migration.** Romania has Europe's largest population of
  *children left at home* (*copii lăsați acasă* — sometimes called
  *euro-orphans*) — estimates vary but multiple NGO reports put the figure
  in the hundreds of thousands. This is a Romania-specific mechanism with
  no direct EU comparator.
- **Seasonal agricultural work.** In rural communities, school attendance
  drops sharply during the agricultural season. The Ministry's own
  reports flag this; World Vision Romania quantifies it in the *Bunăstarea
  copilului din mediul rural* longitudinal series.
- **Early marriage**, concentrated in some Roma communities — see Layer 2.
- **Domestic responsibility for girls** — under-quantified but flagged in
  qualitative NGO research.

QX treats these collectively under "condiții socioeconomice" without naming
them individually. OECD groups them more sharply, with separate sections on
Roma communities, SEN, and rural-urban gaps. The brief follows OECD's
sharper treatment.

## Layer 2 — Roma-specific

The Roma-specific failure is the single sharpest equity gap in the
Romanian system and deserves its own layer rather than being folded into
"socio-economic conditions". The OECD 2025 review's numbers, drawing on
the EU Fundamental Rights Agency 2021 Roma Survey:

- **Over 80% of out-of-school children and youth in Romania are Roma.**
- One in two Roma students aged 6-15 attends a school where the majority
  of students are also Roma — i.e. school segregation, *de facto*.
- **22% of Roma 20-24 year-olds have completed at least upper secondary**,
  compared with 83% in the general population.
- Roughly one-third of rural Romanians self-identify as Roma per FRA
  surveys (with the standard caveats about under-reporting and
  self-identification bias).

The QX report mentions Roma only obliquely, under "etnie" as a
socio-economic factor. This silence is itself worth flagging: Romania's
most marginalised group is not named in the most recent ministerial-level
diagnosis. The brief should be explicit about it.

The mechanism is not mysterious: Roma children enter school with weaker
pre-literacy than non-Roma peers, often attend segregated schools with
weaker teaching resources, encounter limited school-mediator support
(though the ministerial programme has run since 2001), and progressively
disengage. By age 15 most are no longer in school; the EU early-leaver
statistic picks them up at 18-24 as the bulk of the rural component.

The Roma Education Fund, UNICEF Romania, and Salvați Copiii field
operations are the better sources for granular data. Salvați Copiii's
*A Doua Șansă* programme is the operational counter-intervention — see §5.7
case studies and §5.9 recommendations.

## Layer 3 — Demand-side barriers

The OECD 2025 review introduces a layer that QX does not: barriers that
operate even when the school is physically available. Three sub-mechanisms:

- **Financial.** Even with nominally free public education, the marginal
  cost of attendance — transport, school supplies, foregone household
  labour, optional fees — exceeds the threshold many rural and Roma
  families can absorb. The Ministry's social-scholarship programme is the
  policy response; the QX critique that scholarships should be
  administered through *primării* rather than schools is worth weighing
  here.
- **Administrative.** Romania's tertiary admissions process is
  decentralised across universities — each institution has its own
  application timeline, documents, and procedures. The OECD recommendation
  to introduce centralised admissions is direct evidence that this is a
  real barrier, not just an inconvenience. The same point applies to
  vocational and post-secondary applications, which require navigating an
  even less coordinated landscape.
- **Awareness.** A 2021 IRES survey reported by OECD: only about a third
  of Romanian parents understand the concept of early childhood
  education. The take-up of antepreșcolar enrolment is correspondingly
  weak in disadvantaged communities. This is not a poverty effect on its
  own; it is an information failure that compounds poverty.

The OECD recommends information campaigns through school mediators in
disadvantaged communities and through healthcare settings (maternity wards
in particular), citing the French *1000 premiers jours* and the British
Sure Start as models.

## Layer 4 — School-level

The school itself, as a physical and operational entity, is the next layer.
QX is most direct on this:

- **Infrastructure.** As recently as 2025, the ministerial budget had to
  cover "the elimination of all outdoor toilets" — a phrase that should
  not need to appear in a 2025 policy document and that captures the
  state of rural infrastructure better than any aggregate statistic. The
  2025 budget item is real; QX names it.
- **Multi-shift and simultaneous teaching.** Rural schools with too few
  students for a full class often run *clase simultane* — one teacher
  teaching multiple grade levels in the same room. World Vision's 2024
  evaluation of simultaneous teaching documents the achievement penalty.
  Urban schools facing the opposite problem — too many students per
  building — run two or even three daily shifts, with measurable effects
  on instructional time and student fatigue.
- **Teacher absenteeism and vacancy.** Romania does not have a
  sector-wide teacher shortage in headline terms (per OECD 2025, 99% of
  preuniversity teaching staff are formally qualified), but vacancies
  concentrate in rural and disadvantaged schools. The *titularizare*
  system, designed to stabilise teacher placement, has the side-effect of
  routing junior and uncertified teachers into the schools with the
  largest needs. QX's proposed reform of teacher hiring — a national
  licensing exam plus school-level competitions — is aimed at this.
- **Lack of remedial support.** The OECD review and the BRIO numeracy
  report both identify the absence of systematic remediation as a key
  driver of the worsening literacy pyramid across cycles. Students who
  fall behind are not pulled back up; they fall further behind each
  subsequent year.

## Layer 5 — Curricular

A separate mechanism from the school: the official curriculum itself.

- **Overload.** QX is explicit: planurile-cadru (the framework curricula)
  exceed European averages on subject count and instructional hours. The
  proposed reform reduces them toward EU medians and reserves 25% of
  per-subject time for consolidation, remediation, and applied work. The
  high-school *plan-cadru* has been unchanged since 2003 — over two
  decades — despite shifts in pedagogy and labour-market demand.
- **Textbook quality.** QX flags textbook design as "neatractive" and
  recommends redesign combining classical with digital and gaming
  components, plus difficulty-stratified versions for remedial / standard
  / advanced students.
- **Assessment as gatekeeping rather than diagnostic.** Romania's two
  high-stakes national exams — Evaluarea Națională at end of grade 8 and
  the Bacalaureat at end of grade 12 — are designed to sort students,
  not to inform teaching. OECD recommends restructuring grade-8
  assessment to align with the curriculum's actual cross-curricular
  competencies, and QX recommends loosening its selection weight (giving
  individual high schools the option to set up to 50% of their seats via
  their own specialty exams).

The curricular layer interacts with Layer 4. Even a well-designed
curriculum is undeliverable in a school running three shifts with junior
teachers in disadvantaged communities. The brief should resist treating
either layer as a master cause.

## Layer 6 — System

The next layer is system-level: financing, governance, quality assurance,
and inter-ministerial coordination.

- **Chronic underfinancing.** Romania has consistently spent below the
  legal target of 6% of GDP on education for over 15 years. Public
  spending on education, primary to tertiary, is 6.4% of total government
  spending — against an OECD average of 10%. ECEC spending is 1% vs 1.7%
  OECD. The reform target is 15% of total government spending by 2027,
  which would represent a step change — but the trajectory is not yet
  evident in budget allocations.
- **The regressive funding tilt within education.** OECD 2025 makes this
  point sharply: public funding per student in tertiary education is
  almost double ECEC and nearly three times primary. In a country with
  the EU's highest child poverty rate, the funding tilt favours the
  population least in need of public support. The brief takes the OECD
  view here: reallocation matters as much as overall increase.
- **The per-capita financing formula.** Per-student funding penalises
  small rural schools, which have fixed costs that do not scale down with
  enrolment. The result is a structural bias toward closing rural
  schools, which then compounds Layer 1 (distance to school) and Layer 4
  (multi-shift consolidated schools).
- **Weak vocational track.** The 2009 dismantling of *școlile de arte și
  meserii* left a vocational pipeline that absorbs ~14% of eligible
  students and produces the highest dropout rates of any track. The
  ongoing expansion of dual VET (29 PNRR-funded regional campuses) is the
  policy response; evidence of impact is still ahead.
- **Uneven inspectorate and quality-assurance capacity.** ARACIP
  (preuniversity QA) has not historically operated with the same
  standards-driven rigour as ARACIS (higher education). OECD recommends a
  shift from compliance-based to enhancement-based QA; QX recommends
  consolidating the multi-structure inspectorate into a single County
  Direction. The two recommendations are not in conflict and the brief
  should treat them as complementary.

## Layer 7 — Political economy

The deepest layer. The reform measures across Layers 4-6 are not technical
puzzles. They are politically contested.

- **Reform whiplash.** Romania has had 30+ ministers of education in 35
  years. Each minister announces a reform package; almost none survive
  long enough to be evaluated against outcomes. The PISA share below
  Level 2 has been flat since 2006 *because* the reforms intended to
  move it have not been sustained long enough to take effect.
- **Politicisation of the inspectorate.** Inspector general appointments
  follow the political cycle. QX's call for "*politicul se oprește la
  poarta școlii*" is rhetorical but identifies a real mechanism: the
  apparatus that should hold schools accountable is itself accountable
  to the parties that appoint it. The OECD review treats this more
  obliquely (recommending the CSI → County Directorate transition), but
  the underlying critique is the same.
- **Textbook market capture.** Romania's textbook approval process has
  generated multiple public controversies over the past decade about
  which publishers receive contracts and on what criteria. The brief
  does not need to adjudicate specific instances; the systemic point is
  that the textbook market is a rent-extraction site as much as a
  pedagogical one.
- **Reform constituency thinness.** The political constituency for fixing
  rural schools is small. Rural voters skew older; the under-18s the
  reforms would benefit cannot vote. The political constituency for
  preserving the elite track — the *colegii naționale*, the Olympiad
  infrastructure, the urban university hubs — is large, organised, and
  vocal. The path of least political resistance is reform that adds
  resources to the elite track and announces ambitious targets for the
  mass track. This is approximately what has happened across multiple
  reform cycles.

The political-economy layer is the one the brief is most cautious about.
It is the layer where the analysis is most likely to be contested by
ministerial counterparts. It is also the layer where the diagnosis most
matters: if reforms aimed at Layers 4-6 keep failing to move the failure
indicators, the answer is more likely in Layer 7 than in the technical
design of the reforms themselves.

## What the seven layers add up to

Three observations cross the layers:

1. **The causes are tightly coupled.** A rural Roma child living in a
   poverty-stricken household (Layers 1-2) attending a multi-shift
   simultaneous-class school (Layer 4) following a curriculum she cannot
   keep up with (Layer 5), in a county whose inspectorate is run by a
   political appointee (Layer 7), is not failing because of any single
   mechanism. She is failing because all seven layers operate on her
   simultaneously. Interventions on a single layer often have small
   measured effects because the other six layers continue to operate.

2. **The visible interventions are concentrated at Layer 4 and Layer 6.**
   Infrastructure spending, dual VET campuses, teacher training reform,
   curriculum updates — these are real, ongoing, and partly PNRR-funded.
   They are also the layers where political resistance is lowest. Reform
   at Layer 7 is essentially absent from the published reform agenda;
   Layer 1-2 interventions are largely left to NGOs.

3. **The reform package's theory of change is implicit.** Neither QX nor
   the OECD review states it directly, but reading both side by side, the
   theory appears to be: improve Layers 4-6 sufficiently and the Layer
   1-2 disadvantage will be partially offset, opening up the elite track
   to a wider population. The §5.1 finding — PISA share below Level 2
   flat since 2006 — is the empirical test of that theory across two
   decades. So far, the theory has not produced the predicted result.

§5.3 (geography) and §5.7 (case studies) take these mechanisms as input
and trace how they combine in specific places. §5.9 (recommendations)
returns to the layer-mapping and asks which interventions, if any, can
unstick the equilibrium.
